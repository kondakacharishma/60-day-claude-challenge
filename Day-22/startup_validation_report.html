1.Executive Summary

The venture proposes an AI-powered platform that helps students and fresh graduates in India build resumes, prepare for interviews, learn in-demand skills, and receive personalized career guidance. The founder has direct lived experience of the problem, which is a genuine asset — but the venture is pre-launch, with no users, waitlist, or paid pilots yet.

India's graduate employability gap is large and well documented, which supports a real macro-level problem. However, the space is crowded with well-funded incumbents (Internshala, Unstop, Naukri, LinkedIn, Rezi and others), and willingness-to-pay among students specifically has not yet been tested. The core validation risk is not "does the problem exist" — it clearly does — but "will this specific segment pay for a differentiated AI experience, and can a new entrant win distribution against incumbents with existing scale."

2.Problem Validation

India produces roughly 5 million graduates a year against an estimated 2.8 million graduate-level job openings, leaving a significant structural gap. Independent labour-market research has found that roughly two in three unemployed young people in India hold a degree, and separate estimates put unemployment among graduates under 25 at close to 40%. Employability surveys consistently find that a large share of graduates are not considered "job-ready" by recruiters, citing weak interview skills, generic resumes, and a mismatch between classroom learning and hiring requirements.

This confirms the problem is real, large, and persistent at a macro level. The evidence gap is at the micro level: no direct customer interviews, no waitlist, and no usage data have been collected for this specific product concept. Problem existence and product-market desirability are different claims, and only the first is currently evidenced.

6.Market Gap Analysis

  • Fragmentation: students currently stitch together 3–4 separate tools (resume builder + mock interview app + course platform + job board) — no single adaptive journey exists.
  • Personalization depth: most incumbents offer templated resume feedback or generic course catalogs rather than guidance adapted to a student's specific target role and gaps.
  • Tier 2/3 city access: premium career coaching remains concentrated in Tier 1 cities and elite colleges; an AI-first, low-cost model could serve underserved geographies.
  • Vernacular/regional gap: most AI career tools are English-first; a Hindi/regional-language layer is largely unaddressed by current leaders.
Implication: the most defensible wedge is likely "personalized, adaptive guidance for Tier 2/3 students," not competing head-on with Internshala or LinkedIn on breadth.

13.Pivot Opportunities

  • B2B2C via colleges: sell to placement cells/TPOs as an institutional tool rather than direct-to-student, solving distribution and willingness-to-pay simultaneously.
  • Narrow point-solution wedge: launch as an AI mock-interview or resume-scoring tool only, expanding later — lower build cost, faster signal.
  • Vernacular-first positioning: target Tier 2/3, regional-language students as an underserved segment distinct from English-first incumbents.
  • Employer-funded model: recruiters/companies sponsor access in exchange for a qualified, pre-screened candidate pipeline.

14.Go / No-Go Recommendation

CONDITIONAL GO

The underlying problem is real and well-documented at a national scale, and the founder brings genuine, credible motivation and lived experience. However, the venture currently has zero direct customer validation, no defined wedge against a crowded competitive field, and unconfirmed technical execution capacity. Proceeding straight to full-platform build or fundraising at this stage carries high risk of building something the market has not asked for in this exact form.

Condition to convert to a full GO: complete 25–30 problem interviews and a paid pilot (even a manual, non-AI "concierge" version) with at least 50–100 real students within 30 days, demonstrating both real pain and real willingness-to-pay.
